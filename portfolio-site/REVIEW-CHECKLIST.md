# REVIEW-CHECKLIST

## Purpose

- Use this file to review portfolio case studies, portfolio page copy, case summaries, and portfolio presentation copy before shipping.
- This file is for critique and QA. It exists to catch credibility gaps, level mismatch, vague ownership, weak evidence, jargon, process bloat, and low-signal content.
- Use it alongside `PORTFOLIO-BEST-PRACTICES.md`. That file defines the target standard. This file tests whether a draft actually proves it.
- This file is not a writing tutorial, tone guide, or rigid case-study template.

## When to use this checklist

Use this checklist:

- after a first serious draft exists
- before major revision rounds
- before publishing, sharing, or sending portfolio material
- when tailoring content for a specific role or audience
- when an agent is asked to critique, trim, challenge, or revise portfolio content

## Core review principles

- Review for level accuracy first, not surface polish.
- Challenge claims. Clean prose does not rescue weak proof.
- Prefer evidence over assertion.
- Prefer clarity over completeness.
- Prefer judgement over process volume.
- Prefer selective depth over exhaustive chronology.
- Prefer honest caveats over inflated certainty.
- Cut before adding.
- Treat leverage beyond screens as required senior signal, not bonus detail.
- This checklist tests proof, not template compliance.

## Critical fail conditions

A draft is not ready to ship if any of the following are true:

- The reader cannot quickly tell what the work is, why it mattered, and why it belongs in the portfolio.
- The work reads as tactical or mid-level when it is meant to support staff/principal/strategic positioning.
- Role, ownership, or collaboration boundaries are unclear.
- The strategic frame is missing or too weak to explain the stakes, prioritisation, or constraints.
- Leadership, strategy, alignment, or vision language is asserted without mechanism, conflict, or consequence.
- Impact claims are unsupported, inflated, or missing attribution logic.
- The story relies more on brand names, final UI, or polish than on judgement and outcome.
- The main flow is bloated with low-signal process detail, raw artefact dumping, or internal jargon.
- Visual presentation makes the case harder to parse.
- The ending fails to state what changed, what shipped, what was learned, or what remains unresolved.

## Failures especially damaging at staff/principal level

Treat the following as high-risk failures:

- a case that sounds senior but proves only feature execution
- leadership language without mechanism
- collaborative work with blurred authorship
- leverage beyond screens missing from flagship work
- metrics with no believable attribution logic
- process theatre presented as rigour
- old logo-heavy work carrying current credibility

## Severity guidance

- **Must-fix**: blocks shipping. Credibility, level, ownership, evidence, or comprehension failure.
- **Strong suggestion**: materially weakens the case and should normally be fixed.
- **Optional improvement**: useful polish or compression, but not load-bearing.

Resolve must-fix issues before spending time on polish.

## Review categories

Apply highest scrutiny to:

- positioning and relevance
- context and stakes
- strategic framing
- role, ownership, and collaboration clarity
- senior-level signal and leverage
- claims, metrics, and evidence quality
- credibility and authorship

Apply secondary scrutiny to:

- scanability and signal density
- decision quality and tradeoffs
- process selection and process bloat
- language clarity and internal jargon
- ending strength, outcomes, and reflection

Apply supporting scrutiny to:

- repetition and low-signal sections
- visual and presentation quality

## Review questions by category

### 1) Positioning and relevance

- Is it obvious why this case belongs in this portfolio now?
- Does it reinforce the stated positioning or speciality?
- Is it included for evidence rather than brand association or nostalgia?

### 2) Scanability and signal density

- Can a fast scan surface problem, role, stakes, outcome, and contribution?
- Are the most important points visible early rather than buried?
- Does each section earn its space?

### 3) Context and stakes

- Are users, product or business context, timeframe, constraints, and launch status clear?
- Is it clear why the problem mattered and why it was prioritised?
- Does the reader need insider knowledge to understand the setup?

### 4) Strategic framing

- Does the case frame a meaningful product or organisational problem, not just a design task?
- Is it clear what success criteria, strategic stakes, or risks shaped the work?
- Is the strategic frame explicit rather than implied?

### 5) Role, ownership, and collaboration clarity

- Can the reader separate what I owned, influenced, supported, and did not own?
- Is team composition clear enough to calibrate scope?
- Is collaboration visible without hiding authorship inside vague "we" language?

### 6) Senior-level signal and leverage

- Does the case show leverage beyond screens, such as alignment, standards, systems, prioritisation, or organisational change?
- Does it prove operation at the intended altitude?
- If the final UI disappeared, would the case still read as senior?

### 7) Decision quality and tradeoffs

- Is at least one consequential tradeoff explicit?
- Does the case show how user, business, and technical constraints were balanced?
- Is there a real pivot, rejected path, or judgment call?

### 8) Process selection and process bloat

- Does each method or artefact change a decision, reduce risk, or explain collaboration?
- Is the process selective rather than chronological?
- Could major chunks be cut with no loss of reasoning?

### 9) Claims, metrics, and evidence quality

- Are the strongest claims backed by the strongest available evidence?
- Are timeframe, scope, and attribution logic clear?
- Is measured impact separated from inference or shared-team outcome?

### 10) Credibility and authorship

- Does the language sound proportionate, specific, and true?
- Are leadership, strategy, or alignment claims backed by mechanism and consequence?
- Is there any sentence that sounds senior without proving seniority?

### 11) Language clarity and internal jargon

- Could a smart outsider understand this without live explanation?
- Are internal acronyms, programme names, or shorthand translated or cut?
- Is the copy concrete rather than abstract cover language?

### 12) Repetition and low-signal sections

- Are points repeated across intro, body, captions, and ending?
- Are there sections that restate rather than advance?
- What is the first thing to cut if the case must be 30 percent shorter?

### 13) Visual and presentation quality

- Are visuals legible, annotated, and sized for quick understanding?
- Do visuals support a decision, tradeoff, or outcome rather than decorate?
- Are there obvious finishing failures such as typos, broken hierarchy, blurry assets, or awkward cropping?

### 14) Ending strength, outcomes, and reflection

- Does the case end with consequences rather than final UI?
- Is it clear what shipped, what changed, or what was learned?
- Does reflection increase credibility rather than add generic retrospective filler?

## What to flag for revision

Use the following labels when reporting issues:

- **Too tactical**: execution detail outweighs strategic frame, leverage, or consequence.
- **Too vague**: stakes, decisions, role, or outcomes are not precise enough to be credible.
- **Overstated**: the claim outruns the evidence or the actual scope of contribution.
- **Under-explained**: a key decision, tradeoff, or consequence appears without enough context.
- **Polished but unconvincing**: the presentation is clean, but the case still does not prove judgement, ownership, or outcome.
- **Senior-sounding without senior proof**: leadership language is present, but mechanism and evidence are missing.
- **Collaboration blur**: team language obscures actual contribution.
- **Process theatre**: methods and artefacts exceed their decision value.
- **Weak attribution logic**: outcomes are claimed without a believable causal chain.
- **Low-signal repetition**: multiple sections repeat the same point without adding proof.

## What to cut

Cut or de-emphasise the following unless they materially improve understanding:

- duplicated context or outcome paragraphs
- step-by-step process narration
- workshop photos, canvases, or sticky-note walls with no decision value
- low-fi archaeology presented as proof of rigour
- tool mentions that do not change the story
- raw file screenshots or unannotated galleries
- decorative motion, carousels, or interaction that slow scanning
- old brand-name work kept mainly for association
- beauty shots with no annotation
- metrics without scope, timeframe, or attribution logic
- vague collaboration claims such as "worked closely with stakeholders"
- polished filler paragraphs that sound good but prove nothing

## What must be clarified

Clarify the following wherever ambiguity remains:

- why this problem mattered
- why it was prioritised
- who the users or affected audience were
- business, product, or organisational stakes
- role, title, timeframe, and team setup
- direct ownership vs influence vs support
- key constraints
- one consequential tradeoff or pivot
- launch or shipping status
- strongest available evidence and its limits
- what changed because of the work
- why this case belongs in the portfolio now

## Final pre-ship quality bar

A page or case study is ready to ship only if all of the following are true:

- [ ] Level and positioning are clear within a quick scan.
- [ ] Problem, stakes, role, team, constraints, timeframe, and status are explicit.
- [ ] Strategic framing is visible and relevant.
- [ ] Ownership and collaboration are precise and credible.
- [ ] At least one meaningful decision or tradeoff is visible.
- [ ] Flagship work shows leverage beyond screens.
- [ ] Outcomes or consequences are clear.
- [ ] Claims use the strongest available evidence and honest caveats.
- [ ] Process is selective and low-signal detail has been removed.
- [ ] Language is outsider-readable and free of unnecessary internal jargon.
- [ ] Visuals support understanding rather than decoration.
- [ ] The ending states what changed, what was learned, or what remains unresolved.
- [ ] No must-fix issue remains open.

## Final rule

Do not ship a case because it sounds senior.

Ship it only if it proves seniority under scrutiny.
