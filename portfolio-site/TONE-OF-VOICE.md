# Tone of Voice

## Purpose

This file defines how portfolio writing should sound on the page.

Use it to guide drafting and editing at the level of tone, phrasing, cadence, and polish. It should help keep the writing clear, credible, concise, and senior in tone.

This is not a portfolio strategy document, a case study template, an evidence policy, or a review checklist.

## What this file is and is not for

Use this file for:

- tone and register
- sentence and paragraph style
- word choice
- phrasing preferences
- rewriting vague, inflated, generic, or internal-sounding language
- keeping the writing sharp, mature, and high-signal

Do not use this file for:

- deciding what a strong portfolio must prove
- defining case study structure
- setting claims or evidence standards
- running pre-ship critique or QA
- repo workflow or agent instructions

## Audience

The writing is for experienced readers: senior hiring managers, design leaders, founders, and selective peers.

Assume they are intelligent, busy, and scanning quickly. Write for readers who do not need hand-holding, but do need fast clarity.

## Desired impression

The writing should feel:

- strategic
- credible
- concise
- mature
- high-signal
- calm and confident

It should sound polished without sounding performative, direct without sounding blunt, and senior without sounding inflated.

## Core voice traits

The default voice should be:

- **clear**  
  easy to understand on a first pass

- **direct**  
  gets to the point early

- **precise**  
  uses specific language instead of padded abstraction

- **measured**  
  confident, but proportionate to the facts

- **senior**  
  focused on decisions, consequences, and judgment rather than theatre

- **compressed**  
  says enough, then stops

## Priorities in expression

When phrasing choices compete, prioritize in this order:

1. Clarity
2. Credibility
3. Concision
4. Precision
5. Polish

Do not chase elegance at the expense of meaning.

## Language to emphasize

Prefer language that is:

- plain rather than ornamental
- specific rather than broad
- active rather than padded
- concrete rather than theoretical
- decision-oriented rather than process-heavy

Prefer strong verbs over soft framing.

Examples:

- led
- defined
- clarified
- aligned
- simplified
- reduced
- improved
- increased
- enabled
- scaled

## Language to avoid

Avoid language that sounds:

- inflated
- generic
- internal
- self-congratulatory
- overly polished with little meaning
- abstract when a concrete phrasing exists

Avoid weak or padded phrases such as:

- in order to
- through a process of
- leveraged
- holistic
- seamless
- user-centric
- best-in-class
- innovative
- robust
- impactful

Avoid generic UX cover language unless it says something specific:

- user-centered
- intuitive
- frictionless
- delightful
- seamless experience
- improved usability
- enhanced experience

Avoid internal or low-signal habits such as:

- naming rituals instead of decisions
- narrating process step by step
- relying on company shorthand without translation
- using polished strategic language that hides a simple point
- stacking abstract nouns where a verb would be clearer

## Sentence and paragraph guidance

Write in plain English.

Use short to medium sentences most of the time. Vary sentence length when useful, but keep the writing controlled.

Lead with the point of the sentence, not the setup.

Prefer one clear claim per sentence over layered clauses.

Keep paragraphs tight. Most paragraphs should earn their place quickly. Break long blocks before they feel memo-like.

Cut filler transitions unless they add meaning.

Examples:

- Prefer “I simplified the flow and reduced decision friction.”
- Over “In order to improve the overall experience, I worked through a process of simplifying the flow.”

## Altitude and pacing guidance

Default to meaning first.

Get to the core point early. Do not make the reader wait through chronology, scene-setting, or internal process before they understand what matters.

Lead with the most useful layer of information, then add only enough supporting detail to keep the writing credible and readable.

Do not let the prose sink into meeting notes, workshop narration, or internal project history.

## Ownership wording guidance

Use ownership language carefully and plainly.

Use **I** when describing decisions, leadership, authorship, or contributions that were directly yours.

Use **we** when describing shared execution, collective decisions, or team outcomes.

Do not over-claim with **I**. Do not hide behind vague **we** when direct ownership should be clear.

At the sentence level:

- prefer explicit, proportionate ownership
- avoid inflated leadership phrasing
- avoid passive wording that blurs contribution

Examples:

- Better: “I defined the interaction model and worked with engineering to narrow the scope.”
- Weaker: “I drove end-to-end transformation across the experience.”
- Better: “We launched the redesign across core surfaces.”
- Weaker: “I delivered a cross-functional business outcome.”

## Weak phrasing vs stronger phrasing

### Weak

“We ran multiple workshops and validation rounds to align on the solution.”

### Stronger

“We aligned teams around a simpler direction and validated that it improved clarity.”

### Weak

“This initiative focused on creating a more seamless and user-centered experience.”

### Stronger

“This initiative simplified a high-friction experience and made key actions easier to understand and complete.”

### Weak

“I collaborated closely with cross-functional stakeholders throughout the process.”

### Stronger

“I worked across product, engineering, and adjacent teams to align scope, resolve tradeoffs, and move the work forward.”

### Weak

“We explored a number of concepts before landing on the final design.”

### Stronger

“We tested different directions, identified what reduced confusion most effectively, and focused the experience around that.”

### Weak

“The goal was to improve usability and deliver a better experience.”

### Stronger

“The goal was to reduce friction, improve decision-making, and increase confidence in key actions.”

### Weak

“We conducted research and iterated on the experience over time.”

### Stronger

“We used research to identify the main points of confusion, then focused the design on the changes most likely to improve understanding and action.”

## Final note on how to use this file

Use this file while writing and revising.

Ask:

- Does this sound clear, sharp, and credible?
- Is the phrasing doing useful work?
- Is the sentence more polished than informative?
- Does this sound like a senior operator, or like internal documentation?
- Can this be said more directly, more precisely, or with less jargon?

Use this file to improve expression, not to decide the full structure, proof standard, or readiness of the case.
