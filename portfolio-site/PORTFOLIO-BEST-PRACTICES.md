# PORTFOLIO-BEST-PRACTICES

## Purpose

This file defines the content and evidence standards for portfolio site copy, portfolio case studies, and portfolio presentation materials for senior product design opportunities.

It is the governing standard for work that must read as principal, staff, lead, strategic, or senior-level product design.

Use this file during:

- generation
- editing
- review
- pre-ship quality control

This file is not:

- a tone-of-voice guide
- a visual design system
- a writing tutorial
- a personal-branding manifesto
- a workflow or agent-routing document

## Audience

### External portfolio audience

- senior hiring managers
- design directors
- VPs
- founders
- selective peers at staff/principal level
- occasional C-level readers

### Internal audience

- repo maintainers
- writing agents
- coding agents
- review workflows

## Normative terms

- **MUST** = required to ship
- **SHOULD** = preferred default; deviate only with a clear reason
- **MAY** = optional enhancement

## Decision hierarchy

When standards compete, apply them in this order:

1. **Credibility over polish**
2. **Clarity over completeness**
3. **Evidence over assertion**
4. **Relevance over breadth**
5. **Judgement over process volume**
6. **Leverage over screen count**
7. **Usability over clever presentation**

## What strong senior portfolios must prove

A strong senior portfolio MUST prove the following:

1. **Level clarity**
   - The reader can quickly tell that the work reflects senior, staff, principal, or strategic-level thinking.
   - The portfolio does not read like a mid-level feature gallery.

2. **Strategic judgement**
   - The work shows why the problem mattered, how scope was framed, what constraints shaped decisions, and what tradeoffs were made.

3. **Explicit ownership**
   - The reader can tell what was directly owned, what was influenced, what was collaborative, and what was not owned.

4. **Outcome awareness**
   - The work shows what changed, what shipped, what was learned, or what consequence the work had.

5. **Leverage beyond screens**
   - At least some work demonstrates influence beyond interface execution, such as alignment, systems thinking, standards, roadmap shaping, or organisational leverage.

6. **Communication quality**
   - The work is easy to scan, understandable to outsiders, and packaged deliberately rather than dumped from internal artefacts.

7. **Craft threshold**
   - Visual craft, hierarchy, readability, and polish are strong enough to support credibility.
   - Craft alone does not count as senior signal.

## What senior hiring audiences actually care about

### Shared baseline

All senior audiences care about:

- what the problem was
- why it mattered
- what you actually did
- how you made decisions
- how you worked with others
- what changed
- whether the claims are believable

### Hiring managers

Hiring managers care most about:

- role fit
- practical problem-solving under real constraints
- clarity of role and contribution
- evidence that the work shipped or moved something meaningful
- concise explanation

### Design directors and VPs

Design leaders care most about:

- altitude of thinking
- strategic framing
- systems thinking
- cross-functional influence
- product judgement
- leadership leverage
- attention to detail as a proxy for standards

### Founders

Founders care most about:

- speed and prioritisation
- ambiguity handling
- breadth without process theatre
- business traction and product consequences
- ability to create clarity and momentum

### Staff/principal peers

Selective peers care most about:

- level accuracy
- rigor
- honest attribution
- tradeoff quality
- systems thinking
- scalable judgement
- whether the story feels true rather than over-packaged

## What to prioritise

Prioritise the following, in roughly this order:

1. Clear positioning and level signal
2. Significant problems with real stakes
3. Explicit role, ownership, and collaboration boundaries
4. Strategic framing and decision quality
5. Outcome evidence and consequences
6. Leverage beyond screen design
7. Strong scanability and signal density
8. Visual craft and presentation polish

## What to cut or de-emphasise

Cut or de-emphasise the following unless they materially improve understanding:

- internal acronyms, shorthand, and company-specific language
- exhaustive chronological process narration
- workshop theatre
- low-value process artefacts
- duplicated sections that restate the same point
- tool chatter that does not change the story
- speculative impact phrased as fact
- old work included mainly for brand association
- decorative motion or interaction that slows reading
- large galleries of final screens without explanation
- tactical detail that does not support a decision, tradeoff, or outcome
- generic design-thinking boilerplate

## Senior-level signal requirements

### Required core signals in every flagship case

A flagship case MUST show all of the following:

1. **Strategic frame**
   - Why this problem mattered
   - Why it was prioritised
   - What business, user, or product stakes were involved

2. **Explicit contribution**
   - Your role
   - Team structure
   - Scope of ownership
   - Nature of influence

3. **Decision quality under constraints**
   - The major constraints
   - The key decisions
   - At least one meaningful tradeoff or pivot

4. **Outcome or consequence**
   - What shipped, changed, or was learned
   - The strongest available evidence for that outcome

A case that lacks any of the above does not qualify as a flagship senior case.

### Preferred senior leverage signals

A strong staff/principal case SHOULD also show one or more of the following:

- cross-functional alignment
- roadmap or prioritisation influence
- systems or platform thinking
- design standards or design quality leadership
- organisational or process leverage
- ambiguity handling in 0→1 or poorly defined spaces
- mentorship or force-multiplying behaviour
- durable impact beyond the immediate feature

If a case only proves screen-level execution, it should not be treated as a flagship senior case.

### Optional supporting signals

A case MAY include:

- a concise point of view or principle set
- a short methods note where method choice mattered
- links to deeper appendices, decks, or artefacts
- brief reflections on what would be done differently

## Ownership and collaboration rules

Each case MUST make authorship legible.

### Required attribution rules

- State your title or role at the time.
- State the team composition at a functional level.
- State the scope you directly owned.
- State what you influenced but did not directly own.
- Credit collaborators where their work materially shaped the outcome.

### Language rules

- Use **I** for direct ownership.
- Use **we** only for genuinely shared team outcomes.
- Do not use vague collective language to hide unclear ownership.
- Do not use singular ownership language for work that was primarily collaborative.

### Leadership claim rules

Do not claim leadership, strategy, or influence without showing:

- the mechanism
- the decision or alignment moment
- the result or consequence

Claims such as “led strategy”, “drove alignment”, or “shaped vision” MUST be supported by concrete evidence in the case.

## Claims and evidence rules

### Core rule

Every material claim SHOULD be auditable.

### Preferred evidence order

Use the strongest available evidence in this order:

1. confirmed business or user metrics
2. before/after evidence with clear causal logic
3. adoption, launch, rollout, or usage evidence
4. validated learning that changed direction
5. process, standards, or organisational adoption
6. clearly framed directional inference

### Evidence requirements

- Include timeframe where relevant.
- Include scope where relevant.
- Distinguish measured impact from inferred contribution.
- Use caveats when attribution is partial or uncertain.
- If evidence is weak, say so plainly.

### Forbidden evidence patterns

Do not:

- present speculation as outcome
- use numbers without context
- imply sole causation where many factors were involved
- inflate directional movement into proven business impact
- hide missing evidence behind confident language

## Case study guidance at a best-practice level

## Case-study structure rule

A case study MUST satisfy the required signals and content blocks below, but it does **not** need to follow one rigid narrative template.

Strong case studies standardise the essentials and customise the story shape to the work.

### Required case content blocks

Each case MUST include:

1. **Context block**
   - problem
   - users or audience
   - business or product context
   - your role
   - team
   - timeframe
   - constraints
   - launch status

2. **Why this mattered**
   - why the work was important
   - what was at stake
   - why this problem deserved attention

3. **What changed**
   - the core move, outcome, or direction
   - shown early enough that the reader can orient quickly

4. **Decision path**
   - the decision-bearing parts of the work
   - the main tradeoffs
   - the evidence or rationale that shaped direction

5. **Ownership and collaboration**
   - what you owned
   - what was collaborative
   - what you influenced

6. **Outcome and evidence**
   - shipped result, measurable impact, adoption, learning, or organisational consequence

7. **Reflection**
   - what you learned
   - what you would change
   - what remained unresolved, if relevant

### Narrative rules

- Open with context, not suspense.
- Show the outcome early enough to orient the reader.
- Do not bury the point of the case.
- Include enough process to prove judgement, not every ritual.
- Include at least one meaningful tradeoff or pivot.
- Show how user, business, and technical constraints were balanced.
- End with consequence, not just final UI.

### Visual rules

- Annotate visuals so a fast reader knows what matters.
- Use images to support the story, not replace it.
- Do not rely on beauty shots alone.
- Do not include blurry, tiny, or context-free artefacts.
- If a visual does not support a decision, tradeoff, or outcome, cut it.

### Layering rules

- Main case study flow should stay concise and easy to scan.
- Deeper detail MAY live in appendices, linked PDFs, or interview decks.
- The main page should reward skimming first and deeper reading second.

## Portfolio-level guidance at a best-practice level

### Required portfolio-level standards

The portfolio MUST:

- communicate positioning quickly
- expose real work quickly
- make project entry points obvious
- keep navigation simple
- use consistent project labelling
- maintain strong readability and hierarchy
- minimise access friction
- avoid interaction patterns that slow comprehension

### Portfolio selection rules

- Default to a small, curated set of flagship cases.
- More projects are not better by default.
- Include additional projects only if they are equally strong and clearly relevant.
- Prefer launched, recent, representative work.
- If using older work, it MUST still reflect current level and relevance.
- Do not keep weak work for breadth.
- Do not let old logos carry the portfolio.

### Case card or entry-point rules

Project entry points SHOULD communicate enough metadata for quick triage, such as:

- problem or domain
- role
- scope
- outcome type
- why the case is relevant

### Tailoring rules

The portfolio SHOULD allow tailoring by role emphasis:

- more strategic and systems-heavy for principal/staff roles
- more ambiguity, speed, and breadth for founder-facing contexts
- more domain-relevant ordering when targeting a specific opportunity

### Presentation format rules

- Maintain a separate interview deck or equivalent presentation asset.
- Do not rely on the public site alone for high-stakes portfolio reviews.
- Format may vary for very senior, leadership, consulting, or fractional contexts, but the evidence standard does not change.

## Common anti-patterns

### Common anti-patterns in any portfolio

- too much text
- too much process bloat
- final screens without reasoning
- confusing navigation
- weak readability
- blurred hierarchy
- typo-level sloppiness
- jargon that requires insider knowledge
- work that is hard to reach
- visuals with no explanation

### Anti-patterns especially damaging at staff/principal level

- tactical storytelling with no strategic frame
- blurred ownership on collaborative work
- leadership language without proof
- impact claims stronger than the evidence
- workshop theatre presented as judgement
- old brand-name work carrying current credibility
- cases that read like internal documentation
- repeated sections with little new signal
- polished storytelling that feels frictionless or implausible
- jumping between raw files instead of shaping a narrative

## Tradeoff rules

### Process detail vs signal density

Show enough process to prove judgement. Cut the rest. Put deep detail in appendices or presentation materials, not the main flow.

### Polished storytelling vs credibility

Shape the story deliberately, but keep visible tradeoffs, constraints, and unresolved edges. A case should feel composed, not frictionless.

### Visual craft vs business impact

Show both. Do not turn the portfolio into a gallery of polished UI or a text-heavy strategy memo.

### Authorship vs collaboration

Make both visible. Do not erase the team. Do not hide inside “we”.

### Metrics vs evidence quality

Prefer strong evidence, but do not fake precision. Honest caveats are stronger than inflated certainty.

### Standard structure vs case-specific structure

Standardise the required signals and content blocks. Adapt the narrative sequence to the story.

### Confidence vs credibility

Be clear and assertive. Do not oversell, overstate, or imply proof that the case does not contain.

## Quality bar before shipping

A portfolio page or case study is **not ready to ship** if any required item below fails.

### Required to ship

- The reader can understand the role level and positioning quickly.
- The case has a clear context block.
- The strategic importance of the problem is explicit.
- Role, ownership, and collaboration boundaries are explicit.
- At least one meaningful decision or tradeoff is visible.
- Outcome or consequence is stated clearly.
- Claims are supported by the strongest available evidence.
- Internal jargon has been translated or removed.
- The content is easy to scan.
- The page does not read like internal documentation.
- The page does not rely on old logos or polish alone.
- The page is free from obvious proofreading or presentation errors.

### Preferred before shipping

- At least one flagship case shows leverage beyond screens.
- The selected cases reinforce the stated positioning.
- The portfolio can be skimmed quickly and read deeply.
- The story includes reflection or next-step thinking.
- The order of projects is tailored to the target opportunity.

### Optional enhancements

- deeper appendices for interview use
- alternate deck versions for specific roles
- concise principle or point-of-view statements that are proven by the work
- a smaller supporting-project layer beneath the flagship cases

## Final rule

The portfolio should not mainly say, “look what I designed”.

It should say, “here is how I changed the direction, quality, clarity, or outcome of important product work, and here is the evidence.”
