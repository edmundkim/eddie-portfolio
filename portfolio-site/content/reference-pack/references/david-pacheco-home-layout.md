## What I like

- The landing hero uses a solid background color and simple, bold text without extra decoration.
- The case-study navigation anchored at the bottom left feels direct and usable rather than overly styled.
- The top-left utility navigation is minimal and personal: email, LinkedIn, and possibly Instagram.
- The scroll interaction changes the page color as you move into each case, which makes the portfolio feel editorial and alive.
- The ability to lock a work sample or case study is useful for confidential or selective portfolio material.

## What to study

- How the page establishes a strong first impression with very few elements.
- How the fixed navigation and the scrollable case-study entry points coexist without competing.
- How the transition from hero to projects is paced so the layout still feels calm.
- How locked work is signaled clearly without breaking the rhythm of the page.

## Motion / interaction notes

- Case-study entry points should feel scrollable and sequential, not like a static grid of cards.
- Background or section color transitions are a key part of the experience and should help separate projects.
- The clickable project navigation in the lower-left area makes the portfolio feel navigable even before scrolling deeply.
- Motion should stay restrained and structural. The goal is rhythm and orientation, not flashy animation.

## What to avoid copying

- Do not replicate the reference one-for-one in layout details, proportions, or exact interaction timing.
- Do not copy the precise visual identity, typography choices, or personal-navigation structure without adapting them to Eddie's brand.
- Avoid adding Instagram unless it supports the portfolio strategically; the simple nav pattern matters more than the exact channel list.

## Relevance to my portfolio

- This is the strongest current model for transitioning Eddie's portfolio from a more conventional page layout to a scroll-led case-study entry experience.
- It supports a more confident, minimal, design-forward presentation while still keeping the work as the main event.
- The locked-work pattern is especially relevant if some projects need gated or selective access.
