# Overview

## At a glance

New Search was a staged cross-platform reset of search and filters across iOS, Android, desktop web, and mobile web. It was not a cosmetic redesign. It was an attempt to improve a fragmented, high-leverage part of the product while establishing a stronger baseline that could scale across surfaces and support faster future iteration.

The work matters most as a systems story. Search sat close to list-page engagement and lead generation, but the experience had diverged across app and web and had become harder to evolve cleanly. The strategic value was therefore twofold: improve performance where the team could, and simplify the foundation for what came next.

This was also not one neat launch. The work evolved in phases, with different decisions and different levels of proof across platforms:

- iOS became the first clear validation point and the strongest rollout story
- Android showed a narrower but still notable +12% new-user lead signal in Germany rather than a clean cross-market rollout story
- web launched later as a narrower baseline, with richer capabilities intentionally deferred

## Why this mattered

Search and filters were part of a core buyer journey. When that experience is unclear, buyers have a harder time narrowing results, understanding what is applied, and reaching relevant inventory with confidence. That makes the problem more consequential than interface polish.

The challenge was also cross-platform. App and web were not operating as one coherent system. Patterns had diverged, friction showed up differently by surface, and the underlying baseline was harder to improve consistently. That made the work strategically significant: it required deciding where to align, where to diverge, and how to improve a core funnel without forcing the same answer everywhere.

## How the work evolved strategically

The first important move was a reset. Earlier app work did not establish a reliable new baseline, so the team reframed the problem more broadly. Instead of treating search as a single-surface redesign, the work became a cross-platform effort to clarify filtering, reduce friction, and create a better long-term foundation.

iOS became the first strong proof point. A later benchmark established a winning direction, showed a meaningful improvement in lead performance, and led to broad rollout. The signal was positive rather than universal, which is important: this was a credible step forward, not a claim that every metric improved.

Android followed as a narrower proof story than iOS. The clearest supportable highlight is a +12% new-user lead signal in Germany, while leads otherwise appear stable across Germany and Italy in the later summaries. The interaction decision still mattered, but external framing should lead with that measurable signal and treat AutoSave as supporting context rather than the headline. That makes Android a useful example of product judgment under uneven evidence: keep the stronger direction, refine the clarity problem, and avoid overstating what was fully proven.

Web came later and was framed more deliberately as a baseline launch than a finished destination. The team established a simpler search foundation first, accepted a narrower initial scope, and left richer enhancements for later. Desktop showed the clearest positive signal, while mobile web appeared broadly stable. The important point is not that web was feature-complete on day one, but that the baseline had become strong enough to launch and build on.

## Strategic significance

The senior-level value in this work was not just designing new search screens. It was defining a scalable baseline for a fragmented system, linking product quality to commercial outcomes, and making decisions across platforms without flattening real behavioral differences.

The strongest framing for the final case study is therefore:

- a core search experience had become fragmented across app and web
- the work reset that system around a clearer, more scalable baseline
- the team validated the new direction first where proof became strongest
- platform differences were handled through judgment, not forced consistency
- the result was both immediate progress and better leverage for future iteration

New Search should be framed as a cross-platform product-system reset with different proof points by platform, not as one uniform launch or one uniform performance story.
