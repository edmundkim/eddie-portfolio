# Notes

## Initial judgment
- Category: archive
- Confidence: high
- One-line verdict: This shows competent UX thinking around gated partner access and commerce handoff, but it is too campaign-like and too thin on proof to earn a stronger portfolio slot.

## What this project proves
- You can design a partner-facing acquisition flow with multiple entry points and different authentication paths.
- You can turn research into content hierarchy, wireframes, and CTA structure for a focused web experience.
- You can coordinate UX decisions across accessibility requirements and a third-party purchase flow.

## What weakens it
- The scope reads as a promotional landing page, not a substantial product or systems case study.
- There is no concrete outcome evidence beyond vague claims that the experience was simple, secure, and seamless.
- The case is presented as a reinterpretation under confidentiality, which makes the proof feel less direct and less trustworthy.

## Overlap with current portfolio
- It may add a small signal around partner-channel UX, gated entry flows, and commerce integration.
- It likely overlaps with stronger newer work that already shows web UX, collaboration, and structured design process at a more senior level.

## Recommended placement
- Keep it only as a lightweight legacy example if you want one campaign-oriented web project in the archive.
- It belongs in Archive, not Selected Works or Earlier Work.
- It is only worth further rewriting if you specifically need the partner-authentication signal.

## Rewrite direction
- Reframe it around the core UX problem: one secure experience serving two partner groups with different access models.
- Compress the brand setup and generic process description; sharpen the constraints, decisions, and flow logic.
- Lead with the authentication approach, content strategy, accessibility standard, and third-party purchase handoff.

## Open questions
- Did this actually launch, and were there any measurable results such as leads, bookings, or conversions?
- How much of the final interaction model and shipped UI was directly yours versus shared across UX, creative, and development?
