# Supporting Case Draft

## Title
Daimler Vehicle Subscription Concept

## Positioning summary
This project matters as a compact example of zero-to-one service design under ambiguity. The strongest signal is not final UI polish, but the ability to turn a vague vehicle subscription concept into a clearer acquisition and lifecycle journey, then pressure-test that structure with users and senior client stakeholders.

## The problem
Daimler was exploring a new vehicle subscription offer, but the model itself was unfamiliar and the business requirements were still taking shape. The challenge was not only to design a checkout flow, but to make the service understandable from vehicle selection through delivery and ongoing lifecycle management within a two-month concept engagement spanning desktop and mobile web.

## My role
I was the Experience Design Lead at Deloitte Digital Germany. I owned the UX definition work: research synthesis, competitive review, journey and flow definition, wireframes, and presentations to Daimler and Mercedes-Benz executives. I worked alongside a strategist, systems architect, UI designer, and design leadership, so the business model, visual design, and downstream MVP delivery were shared or outside my direct ownership.

## What I shaped
- The end-to-end journey from vehicle selection and subscription configuration through account setup, verification, delivery, and lifecycle management.
- A lower-friction acquisition flow that reduced upfront data entry, used a focused step-by-step configuration approach, and kept a clear subscription summary visible during decision-making.
- Testing-informed refinements around navigation clarity, payment and review sequencing, policy visibility, step progression, and how lifecycle actions were explained after sign-up.

## Why it mattered
Vehicle subscription sat between ownership, leasing, and service, which made the UX burden unusually high: people first had to understand what they were buying and how the relationship would work over time. Giving that model a clearer structure helped the client align around a more credible concept and provided useful input for follow-on work, even though this phase did not produce market-facing impact data.

## Constraints and truth boundaries
- This was a two-month greenfield concept engagement, not a shipped public product or launched MVP.
- The strongest defensible outcomes are client approval, user-testing feedback from seven relevant participants, and reported influence on later work.
- I can credibly claim UX definition and senior client-facing design leadership here, but not sole ownership of the business model, visual execution, or any later delivery outcomes.

## Closing takeaway
As Earlier Work, this adds a useful signal that the stronger current portfolio pieces may not show as directly: structuring an unfamiliar service model end to end under ambiguity and executive scrutiny. It works as a supporting case because the judgment is credible and the scope is meaningful, but the evidence remains too concept-heavy for flagship treatment.
