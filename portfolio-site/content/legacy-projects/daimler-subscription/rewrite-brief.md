# Rewrite Brief

## Recommendation
- Status: proceed
- Placement: Earlier Work
- Format: compact supporting case
- Confidence: high

## Core story
- One-sentence case for why this project deserves to exist in the portfolio: It shows you can turn a vague, net-new vehicle subscription model into a clearer end-to-end acquisition and lifecycle experience under tight timelines and senior client scrutiny.
- Primary angle: Making an unfamiliar vehicle subscription model understandable and lower-friction across the full journey from selection to lifecycle management.
- Secondary angle: Senior UX definition and stakeholder alignment across research, flows, wireframes, and testing in a compressed consulting engagement.
- What this project most credibly proves: You can structure an ambiguous service concept into a testable cross-channel journey without relying on generic process language.

## What must be true in the rewrite
- This was a two-month greenfield concept engagement, not a shipped public product or an MVP you took to market.
- Your ownership stays within UX definition: research, landscape review, flows, wireframes, presentations, and collaboration with strategy, architecture, UI design, and leadership.
- Any outcome claim must stop at client approval, user-testing feedback, and helping inform follow-on work; it cannot imply direct market impact, shipped success, or sole credit for winning the MVP.

## What to avoid
- Framing it as "the future of mobility" or any other inflated trend story that says little about your actual contribution.
- Suggesting the concept itself proves product-market success, operational viability, or strong business impact.
- Treating it like flagship material, a Selected Works case, or a deep hero case study.
- Repeating generic consulting-process narration like double diamond, lean process, or broad workshop language without tying it to a concrete decision.

## Strongest usable proof points
- The UX problem is specific and credible: make an unfamiliar subscription model understandable from vehicle selection through checkout, delivery, and lifecycle management.
- The scope is senior enough to matter: two months, desktop and mobile web, cross-functional collaboration, and direct presentations to Daimler and Mercedes-Benz executives.
- The concept was tested with seven relevant participants, which produced concrete adjustments around navigation, payment/review order, clearer step progression, policy clarity, and lifecycle communication.

## Weak or missing evidence
- There is no shipped-product result, adoption data, or real business outcome beyond client approval and reported influence on follow-on work.
- The validation is directionally useful but light for a senior case: seven participants, broad success language, and a 4.5/5 rating.
- It is still unclear which parts of the final MVP carried forward and which structural decisions are most distinctly yours versus shared team output.

## Recommended structure for the supporting case
1. The Problem — define the unfamiliar subscription model, the acquisition-plus-lifecycle scope, and the ambiguity in the business requirements.
2. What I Shaped — clarify your role, the team setup, and the specific parts of the journey you led.
3. Key UX Decisions — show the few structural decisions that made the service easier to understand and lower-friction.
4. Outcome and Limits — state the testing takeaways, client approval, and the honest boundary that this remained concept work.

## Rewrite priorities
- Lead with: the unclear subscription model, the end-to-end service scope, and your role in making it understandable.
- Compress or cut: trend framing, generic process sections, image-caption filler, and any long narration of standard UX activities.
- Clarify: what decisions you personally drove, what testing actually changed, and how confidently you can describe the concept's influence on the follow-on MVP.
- Save for archive if needed: detailed competitor teardown, full process timeline, and extra UI screens that do not strengthen the core argument.

## Suggested length and depth
- Homepage/supporting card summary: 25-35 words
- Internal supporting case: 400-600 words
- Should this become a full case study page? no
- Why: The signal is real, but the evidence is too conceptual and outcome-light to justify flagship treatment or a long-form case study.

## Open questions
- How much of the funnel, lifecycle, or information architecture actually survived into the MVP after Deloitte won the follow-on work?
- Can you identify two or three specific structural decisions that were clearly yours and are still visible in the surviving artifacts?

## Final call
- Why this project is worth carrying forward now: It is worth carrying forward only as a compact Earlier Work supporting case because it adds distinct zero-to-one mobility and service-lifecycle signal without justifying flagship emphasis.
- Biggest risk in rewriting it: It can easily read as polished concept theater if the rewrite leans on vision language or indirect outcomes instead of concrete UX decisions and evidence.
- Best next step after this brief: Draft a short supporting case outline capped at 400-600 words around the problem, your role, three key decisions, and the narrow outcome claims you can defend.
