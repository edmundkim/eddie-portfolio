# Legacy Project Ranking

## Ranking criteria
Use these criteria when comparing the projects:
- Distinct signal added to the portfolio
- Strength of outcomes or evidence
- Clarity of my role and ownership
- Seniority / strategic weight
- Redundancy with my current stronger work
- Effort required to rewrite into a strong supporting piece
- Likelihood that the project improves the portfolio rather than dilutes it

## Side-by-side assessment

### Daimler Subscription
- Distinct signal: 4
- Outcome strength: 3
- Role clarity: 4
- Seniority / strategic weight: 4
- Redundancy with current portfolio: 3
- Rewrite effort required: 3
- Final category: keep now
- Why: This is the cleanest legacy story in the set: a greenfield subscription model, end-to-end lifecycle scope, and credible senior stakeholder exposure. The outcome proof is still lighter than ideal, but the role is clearer than in the other cases and the UX problem is specific enough to rewrite without inventing substance. It adds zero-to-one service-design signal without collapsing into a generic consulting-process story.

## Ranked order
1. Daimler Subscription — Only project with a clear enough role, specific problem, and manageable rewrite path to justify carrying forward now.
2. James Hardie — Broad enterprise and systems signal makes it the best backup if you want non-automotive legacy breadth.
3. TELUS Mobile Strategy — Strategically interesting, but too abstract and recommendation-heavy to prioritize above the top tier.
4. Questrade — Useful IA/content-systems signal, though it lands more as planning rigor than compelling product impact.
5. Smart Connected Car App — High complexity but too redundant with stronger current automotive work and too light on launch evidence.
6. Kimono Design System — Decent craft artifact, but weak ownership and adoption make it a low-value rewrite.
7. Genesis Landing Page — Narrowest scope and weakest proof, so it is the easiest project to cut.

## Recommended survivors
- Survivor 1: Daimler Subscription
Why it survives: It combines zero-to-one service design, cross-channel journey work, and reasonably clear stakeholder-facing ownership in one story.
Best placement: Earlier Work or a compact Supporting Work slot, not Selected Works.
Rewrite priority: High, but only as a short, disciplined case with tighter evidence.
- Survivor 2: none
Why it survives: No other project clears the bar strongly enough to spend rewrite time on right now.
Best placement: Keep James Hardie as the first archive piece to revisit only if you later need a non-automotive enterprise example.
Rewrite priority: None for now.

## Archive candidates
- James Hardie — Good enterprise/B2B2C systems breadth, but too diffuse and outcome-light to prioritize now.
- TELUS Mobile Strategy — Strong strategy/governance signal, but too abstract and too weakly evidenced for a front-line slot.
- Questrade — Useful IA/content-system proof, though it lands more as approved planning than as shipped impact.

## Drop candidates
- Smart Connected Car App — Overlaps too much with stronger current automotive work and has no public-launch proof.
- Kimono Design System — Contributor-level signal and thin adoption evidence make it a poor use of limited portfolio space.
- Genesis Landing Page — Campaign scope plus weak outcomes make it the weakest project in the set.

## Final recommendation
- Maximum number of legacy projects to carry forward now: 1
- Recommended structure: Keep one compact legacy survivor in Earlier Work, keep two or three archive-only backups offline, and drop the rest from active portfolio planning.
- Biggest risk if I bring over too many: The portfolio starts reading like a backlog of respectable consulting projects instead of a tight senior narrative with strong current proof.
- Clear next step: Rewrite Daimler Subscription into a short supporting case, then reassess whether there is a real gap that James Hardie or Questrade uniquely fills.
